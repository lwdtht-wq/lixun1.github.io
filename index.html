Mosaic of Faith: The Distribution of Religious Beliefs in Singapore

Background

Singapore is a multicultural and multi-ethnic society where religion plays an important role in shaping both social structures and cultural identity. Different religions not only influence people’s lifestyles, festivals, and values but also shape the diversity of public spaces (e.g., temples, mosques, and churches coexisting). However, with globalization and secularization, the structure of religious belief is changing—for example, the rise of the “no religion” group among younger generations.

Research Objectives

  • To outline the distribution and proportion of major religious groups in Singapore (e.g., Buddhism, Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, Taoism).
  • To explore the role of religion in public life, festivals, and community activities.
  • To analyze the influence of different religious groups on social interaction and cultural identity.
  • To compare differences in religious beliefs across age groups and ethnic communities.

Research Questions

  • What are the major deities or religions that Singaporeans believe in?
  • Is the distribution of religious believers related to ethnicity, language, or historical background?
  • What are the differences in religious beliefs between younger and older generations?
  • How does religious diversity affect Singapore’s social cohesion?

Research Methods

  • Document Analysis: Collect census data on religion from the Singapore Department of Statistics (SingStat), along with academic research and government publications.
  • Interviews & Observation: Visit different religious sites (temples, mosques, churches) and engage with believers and religious representatives.
  • Case Studies: Select key religious festivals (e.g., Thaipusam, Hari Raya Puasa, Chinese New Year) and analyze their role in Singapore’s social life.

Expected Outcomes

  • A systematic overview of the distribution of religions in Singapore, highlighting diversity.
  • A cultural understanding report explaining how religion influences festivals, food habits, and everyday interactions.
  • A comparative perspective revealing generational and group-based differences in religious views.
  • Educational significance: a reference for understanding Singapore’s plural society and intercultural exchange.

Potential Significance

  • Contributes to fostering mutual understanding and respect among different religious groups.
  • Provides valuable material for intercultural education and social research.
  • Highlights Singapore’s unique position as a diverse society in terms of religious belief.
